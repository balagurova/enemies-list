Political prisoners

Political prisoners in today's Russian Federation are very different people. There are people imprisoned because of who they are (sexual orientation, ethnic, national, social origin etc.) or what they believe (religious, political or other conscientiously held beliefs). There are civil and political activists persecuted for their activities, and there are those who were imprisoned for participating in scientific exchange of information or reposting on a social network. There are people who have literally become hostages, that is, imprisoned because they refused to stipulate others or in order to influence their relatives and friends, but there are completely accidental victims of the state repressive mechanism.

One of the story is DOXA which is the most well-known student publication in Russia. Four young journalists faced three years in jail for supporting students during peaceful protests. They were being prosecuted to end their activities as independent journalists and critics of the current government, as well as intimidating society as a whole and preventing citizens, and especially young people, from exercising their inalienable political rights. Currently, the whole DOXA stuff is under house arrest, awaiting a trial. All they have done was to do their job.

Undesirable organisations

Undesirable organisations, according to the federal law, include foreign civil society organisations which represent a threat to the foundations of the constitutional order and security of the Russian Federation. This status means a total ban on their work in the country and any cooperation with them by Russian organisations and citizens under a threat of criminal persecution. In recent years, the human rights based work of People in Need, the European Platform for Democratic Elections, the Prague Civil Society Center and the Association of Schools of Political Studies of the Council of Europe has already been prohibited in the country.

A prominent investigative news website Proekt had to suspend operations immediately when it became the first media outlet which was added to the list of “undesirable organisations.“ The Russian State chose the moment when the Project leader, Roman Badanin was abroad and opened a criminal investigation against him. That is how they banish people from the country even if the Constitution expressly forbids it. The Project team promised to continue their investigations. “And once again we thank everyone for their support. Journalism is not a crime!"

Foreign agents

This list includes organizations or individuals engaging in political activity and receiving foreign funding. Once placed on the list, organizations and individuals are required to make financial disclosures four times a year detailing all sources of income and spendings. They must also append a disclaimer to all publications and social media posts identifying themselves as foreign agents. Failure to do so is punishable by fines or sentences. Many organisations suppose that the law on “foreign agents” violates both Russia’s Constitution and the European Convention on Human Rights.

No to Violence (Nasiliu.net) offers assistance and support to victims of domestic violence in Russia. The decision is based on the fact that the organizations criticized the persecution of non-profit organizations, wrote about the persecution of LGBT people and advocated the adoption of a law on domestic violence. NGO’s co-founder Anna Rivina shared her feelings about the decision: «I was insanely hurt. In general, this does not happen to me, but when I would start talking, I would immediately cry. <…> I know for sure that I am not a “foreign agent”,I do nothing in the interests of another state, I work in the interests of myself as a citizen of thecountry and people who come to us for help every day.»
